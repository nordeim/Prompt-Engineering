### Part 3: The Design Prompt Library

Use these prompts at the start of a project to force intentional, anti-generic thinking. They are designed to be provocative and to prevent convergence on safe, templated solutions.

#### **On First Principles**
- "If our brand were a person, would they be a tenured professor or a startup founder?" (This sets the tone for everything that follows.)
- "Does our user need a handshake or a high-five?" (Handshake = trust, formality. High-five = energy, camaraderie.)
- "Are we selling a safe harbor or a rocket ship?" (Safe harbor = protection, stability. Rocket ship = journey, transformation, risk/reward.)

#### **On Typography**
- "If our headlines were spoken, would they be whispered or shouted?" (Whisper = refined, confident, understated. Shout = bold, declarative, attention-grabbing.)
- "Is our typeface a workhorse or a show pony?" (Workhorse = maximum utility, readability across contexts. Show pony = distinctive, used sparingly for impact.)
- "What would our typography look like if it had to communicate without any words?" (Focus on scale, weight, texture, and color as graphic elements.)

#### **On Color**
- "If our brand had a flavor, would it be mineral water or an energy drink?" (Mineral water = clean, pure, neutral, premium. Energy drink = vibrant, intense, stimulating.)
- "Does our color palette make the user feel calm or courageous?" (Calm = low saturation, blue/green families. Courageous = high saturation, purple/red/orange families.)
- "If we removed all color, would our design still communicate our brand?" (If the answer is no, you're using color as a crutch, not a strategic tool.)

#### **On Layout**
- "Does our grid feel like a comfortable home or an exciting city?" (Home = predictable, safe, known. City = dynamic, layered, full of discovery.)
- "If our page were a room, would the furniture be against the walls or floating in the middle?" (Against walls = traditional, formal, safe. Floating = modern, dynamic, confident.)
- "Where do we want the user's eye to *struggle*?" (This is a paradoxical but powerful question. Sometimes you want a moment of visual tension—a place where the eye pauses and engages, rather than gliding smoothly past.)

#### **On Imagery & Interaction**
- "Do we show the user what they will become, or how they will feel when they get there?" (Show the *result* vs. evoke the *emotion*.)
- "If our site made a sound, would it be a library or a concert hall?" (Library = quiet, focused, respectful. Concert hall = energetic, immersive, communal.)
- "Does our animation serve the story or just decorate the page?" (If it's decoration, delete it.)

### Part 5: Final Synthesis — The Universal Truths

Across both sites, four universal truths emerge that apply to *any* project, regardless of your chosen position on the spectrum.

#### **Truth 1: Intentionality is the Only Differentiator.**
`itrust` is not "boring." It is *intentionally* restrained. `kimi.link` is not "flashy." It is *intentionally* bold. The worst possible state is an unintentional design—a site that is neither clearly trustworthy nor clearly innovative, a victim of default choices and template thinking.

#### **Truth 2: Hierarchy is a Sacred Duty.**
Both sites, despite their visual differences, have impeccable hierarchy. In `itrust`, it's achieved through predictable scale and spacing. In `kimi.link`, it's achieved through dramatic contrast and isolation. But in both, the user *never* wonders what to look at next. Hierarchy is not about making things "look nice"; it's about **managing the user's attention as a finite resource**.

#### **Truth 3: Whitespace is a Voice, Not an Absence.**
In `itrust`, whitespace speaks of organization and calm. In `kimi.link`, it speaks of premium isolation and drama. Never treat whitespace as "empty space to fill." Treat it as a deliberate structural material that communicates as powerfully as any typography or image.

#### **Truth 4: Accessibility is a Mark of Mastery.**
Both sites, in their own ways, demonstrate that great designers engineer for inclusion. `itrust` does it through inherent simplicity. `kimi.link` must do it through rigorous engineering. But neither uses complexity as an excuse for exclusion. A commitment to accessibility is a commitment to craft.
